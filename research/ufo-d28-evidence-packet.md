## Bottom Line

`D28` remains a high-value document-only case, not a video-correlated case. The strongest preserved claim is narrow: during an armed overwatch / weapons-calibration mission on `2024-09-20`, after an AGM-176 release and before munition impact, the crew reported an unidentified object crossing the aircraft sensor field, producing IR lens flare on `MX-20` and `MX-25` sensors.

The report is valuable because it gives an exact initial contact DTG, named sensor systems, a constrained mission phase, and negative controls. It is weak for physics because the public release does not preserve object speed, range, altitude, trajectory, size, raw imagery, sensor pointing, field of view, or munition release/impact timestamps.

Treat the event as Iraq / Ayn Al Asad ROZ Raindrop. The file name and PDF metadata say East China Sea, but the body is consistently USCENTCOM / Operation Inherent Resolve / OKAS / Ayn Al Asad.

## Claim Tiers

### Directly Preserved

- The event was recorded in an official MISREP and declassified by USCENTCOM.
- The report body places the mission in USCENTCOM / Operation Inherent Resolve, not in the East China Sea.
- The UAP event was logged at `202027:59ZSEP24`.
- The report identifies `MX-20` and `MX-25` as the sensors that detected the IR/lens-flare signature.
- The event occurred after weapons release and before munition impact.
- The report states no observed response, no equipment/person effects, no recovered material, and no reported additional aircraft in the relevant airspace.

### Reporter Characterization

- "Significant heat source" is the reporter's interpretation of the IR lens flare.
- "High rate of speed" is a qualitative characterization; no public measured speed is preserved.
- "Path of movement appeared predetermined" is an observer assessment, not a reconstructed trajectory.
- The possible object detachment is explicitly uncertain.

### Missing or Redacted

- UAP speed, altitude, depth, and trajectory fields are blank despite being marked estimated.
- UAP first/last seen detailed coordinates are redacted or blank.
- Object count is not preserved in the structured field.
- No public raw video, still frame, telemetry, sensor pointing, FOV/zoom state, slant range, munition release timestamp, or munition impact timestamp is attached.
- No public PR/DVIDS video pairing has been found.

## Analytic Controls

D28 should be evaluated against at least four conventional or non-extraordinary lanes before any exotic interpretation:

1. Active weapons-employment optical effects, including flare or glare in the targeting/sensor chain.
2. Munition, plume, fragment, debris, or other range activity crossing a sensor field during the shot.
3. Sensor or display artifact associated with IR optics, laser designation, platform motion, or sensor handoff.
4. Another aircraft or object not reported by third parties but still not resolved in the released record.

The report's negative controls matter, but they do not close these lanes because the released record lacks the raw sensor data and geometry.

## Follow-Up Data Needed

To promote D28 beyond document-only status, the needed material is specific:

- `MX-20` and `MX-25` imagery or video around `202027:59ZSEP24`.
- Weapon release and impact timestamps.
- Sensor pointing, FOV/zoom, range, and platform attitude at the event time.
- Platform position and target/munition geometry.
- Any AARO case notes explaining the lens-flare interpretation.
- Any official PR/DVIDS page that names `D28`, `202027ZSEP2024-CENTCOM`, `AGM-176`, `MX-20`, `MX-25`, or Ayn Al Asad.

## Current Judgment

D28 is one of the best document-only records because it preserves mission context, sensor names, event timing, and negative controls. It is not presently one of the best public video cases and should not be used for speed, size, or maneuver claims until paired imagery or telemetry appears.
