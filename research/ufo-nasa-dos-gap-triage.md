## NASA Findings

### Apollo 12

The Apollo 12 transcript is not a clean unknown-object case. The strongest source language describes lights or particles seen in the dark quadrant and bits/pieces near the spacecraft. The transcript itself includes mundane mission-context anchors: water boiler speculation, tracking-light discussion, floating material near the spacecraft, and instrument/EMI discussion. Treat as astronaut observational context, not as a physical UAP case.

The Apollo 12 photos (`VM1-VM5`) are visually interesting only at catalog level in this pass. The release images contain highlighted regions above the lunar horizon, but the public descriptions state that the highlights are contextual aids and do not constitute analytical or factual determinations. Without original unannotated scans, camera metadata, star-field calibration, and defect analysis, the photos should not be used for strong object claims.

### Gemini 7

The Gemini 7 transcript preserves a reported "bogey at ten o'clock high" and a question from Houston about whether it was the booster or a natural sighting. The same transcript immediately includes booster-in-sight and particle/debris context, including many small particles and a brilliant body in sunlight. Treat as an ambiguous spaceflight observation in a debris/booster environment.

### Apollo 17

Apollo 17 has the strongest NASA narrative lane, but it is still conventionally bounded. The transcript describes bright fragments, jagged/angular particles, a flashing distant object, and later discussion that the object may be the S-IVB, SLA panels, or related mission hardware. The Apollo 17 image (`VM6`) shows three highlighted lights in triangular arrangement above lunar terrain, but the public release does not provide enough photogrammetry, original negative/scan metadata, exposure context, or independent calibration to determine object nature.

The Apollo 17 technical/science debrief files are mixed. `D5` is mostly scientific background on unexpected UV/X-ray/astronomical measurements, not a UAP object report. `D6` preserves dark-adapted light flashes and one possible lunar-surface flash, but light flashes in astronaut visual systems have strong conventional spaceflight/particle/radiation context and should not be promoted as external object evidence without more data.

### Skylab

The Skylab debrief is useful as a control. It explicitly discusses light flashes, possible cosmic-particle visual effects, a bright reddish object that looked like a satellite in a similar orbit, and other lights presumed to be Skylab pieces or satellites. The public text repeatedly frames these sightings in conventional orbital-environment terms.

## DOS Findings

### Papua New Guinea 1985

The PNG cable is a diplomatic inquiry, not a resolved UAP case. It reports local concern over high-altitude/high-speed objects, an Air Niugini pilot radar report near Angoram, visual contrail reports, and an embassy statement that records/telcon found no known B-52 or U.S. aircraft overflights. The cable asks for confirmation or explanation. It is useful as a historical diplomatic-reporting lane but too sketchy for physical conclusions.

### Kazakhstan / Tajik Air 1994

The Tajik Air cable is the strongest non-DoD narrative reviewed in this pass. It reports that American pilot Ed Rhodes and colleagues, flying a Boeing 747SP at `41,000 feet` over Kazakhstan, observed a bright object for about `40 minutes`, with reported circles, corkscrews, 90-degree turns, high speed, and later contrails estimated around `100,000 feet`.

The source also preserves the key limiting structure: the extraterrestrial/intelligent-control language is reported as Rhodes's opinion, not a State Department finding. The cable closes with the embassy comment that it had no opinion and was reporting the matter for what it was worth. This case is high-interest witness/diplomatic reporting, not a sensor-verified physics case.

## Analytic Treatment

Promote this group from inventory-only to targeted triage, but do not promote any NASA/DOS case above the modern operational DoW/DoD evidence ladder.

Recommended treatment:

- `DOS D2` becomes the strongest historical witness/diplomatic narrative lane.
- `NASA D2/VM6` becomes the strongest NASA spaceflight visual/transcript lane.
- `NASA D7` becomes a useful control lane because it explicitly discusses satellites, debris, and cosmic-particle-like light flashes.
- `DOS D1` remains a sketchy diplomatic inquiry lane.
- Apollo 12 and Apollo 11 records remain context lanes with strong mission-hardware, particles, reflection, or visual-phenomenon caveats.

## Current Judgment

This pass adds historical breadth but does not change the final scientific report's main conclusion. The modern operational DoW/DoD records remain stronger because they preserve structured mission fields, sensor context, and public release pairings. NASA/DOS records add interesting historical witness and spaceflight context, but they do not provide raw telemetry or calibrated imagery sufficient for extraordinary-origin or physical-performance claims.
